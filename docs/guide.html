Black Hole Explorer

A scientifically accurate, real-time relativistic black-hole simulator — ray-traced curved spacetime, a relativistic accretion disk, live physics in real units, and the science of what we actually know.

WebGL2 · zero dependencies Grounded in EHT & GR GPU-adaptive Material 3 × Apple HIG

Launch & stop

  1. Easiest: double-click index.html — it opens in your browser. To close, just close the tab.
  2. Recommended: double-click start.bat — a tiny local server opens it at http://localhost:8765. To stop: close the tab, then press Ctrl+C in the little black server window.
  3. Any OS: run python serve.py in the folder, then open http://localhost:8765.

Needs a browser with WebGL2 (Chrome, Edge, Firefox, Safari 15+). On a gaming laptop, see USE_NVIDIA_GPU.md to put it on your dedicated GPU.

The science — what's happening & what we know

Event horizon & shadow

The horizon is the point of no return — a one-way surface where escape velocity equals light speed. It's invisible; what we see is the larger dark shadow, ~2.6× wider, cast on surrounding light.

rₛ = 2GM/c² · shadow = 6√3·GM/c² ≈ 5.2 rₛ rₛ = 2.95 km × (M/M☉)

Photon sphere & photon ring

At 1.5 rₛ light can orbit. Photons that loop before escaping form the razor-thin photon ring — a stack of self-similar sub-rings, each ≈ e−π ≈ 1/23 fainter. A near-pure prediction of general relativity.

Turn on Photon paths (Geometry & overlays) to watch it directly: rays with impact parameter b < b_c ≈ 2.6 rₛ are captured, rays near b_c orbit the photon sphere, and rays with b > b_c escape with a deflection. That critical impact parameter is exactly what sets the edge of the shadow.

r_ph = 3GM/c² = 1.5 rₛ · b_c = 3√3·GM/c² ≈ 2.6 rₛ

The accretion disk

Infalling gas forms a hot disk, glowing as a multi-temperature blackbody — bluest at the inner edge (the ISCO), redder outward (T ∝ r−3/4). Peak T ∝ M−1/4: stellar holes blaze in X-rays, giants in UV/optical.

T(r) = [3GMṀ/(8πσr³)·(1−√(r_in/r))]^¼

Doppler beaming & redshift

The inner disk orbits at ~0.5c, so the approaching side is boosted brighter & bluer, the receding side dimmed & reddened — a lopsided bright crescent. Light climbing out is gravitationally redshifted too.

δ = 1/[γ(1−β·cosθ)] · I_obs ∝ δ³

Spin, Kerr & the ergosphere

Real holes rotate (a* = 0–1). Spin shrinks the horizon, splits it in two, and turns the singularity into a ring. Outside lies the ergosphere, where spacetime is dragged so hard nothing can stand still — frame dragging.

r₊ = M(1+√(1−a*²)) · r_E(θ) = M(1+√(1−a*²cos²θ)) ISCO: 6GM/c² (a=0) → 1GM/c² (extremal prograde)

Time dilation & spaghettification

Clocks slow deeper in the well; at the horizon they appear (to us) to freeze, so infalling matter never seems to cross. Tidal forces stretch you into a strand — and smaller holes are deadlier, since the gradient scales as 1/M².

dτ/dt = √(1−rₛ/r) · tidal Δa = 2GML/r³

Hawking radiation, entropy & the singularity

Quantum effects make holes glow faintly — hotter when smaller — and slowly evaporate (lifetime ∝ M³). Their entropy scales with horizon area, seeding the holographic principle. Inside lies a singularity, and the unsolved information paradox.

T_H = ħc³/(8πGMk_B) · S = k_B A c³/(4Għ) T_H = 6.17×10⁻⁸ K ×(M☉/M) · lifetime ≈ 2.1×10⁶⁷ yr ×(M/M☉)³

How we image them (EHT)

The Event Horizon Telescope made an Earth-sized virtual telescope (VLBI at 1.3 mm) to resolve the ring: M87* (2019) and Sgr A* (2022). Despite a ~1500× mass difference they look nearly the same size from Earth — mass and distance almost cancel.

θ_g[µas] = 0.0322 × (M/M☉)/(D/ly) M87* ring 42±3 µas · Sgr A* ring 51.8±2.3 µas

Performance

The renderer detects your GPU and shows a tier badge. Auto quality continuously scales render resolution & ray-march steps to hold a target frame-rate; it pauses when the tab is hidden and caps the frame-rate to stay cool. On a strong GPU pick Ultra (1.35× supersampling); on a weak one pick Battery.

On hybrid-graphics laptops the browser is often forced onto the integrated GPU — a banner offers one-click guidance, detailed in USE_NVIDIA_GPU.md.

Sources & honesty

Built on standard general relativity & astrophysics: Schwarzschild & Kerr metrics, Bardeen–Press–Teukolsky (1972) ISCO, Shakura–Sunyaev (1973) / Novikov–Thorne disks, Hawking (1975), Luminet (1979), James–Thorne (2015, the Interstellar renderer), and EHT (2019/2022). Full derivations & citations are in PHYSICS.md.

Honest limitations: photon paths use the Schwarzschild metric (spin drives the disk's inner edge, ergosphere & readouts, but not full Kerr photon frame-dragging — so the shadow stays circular); the disk is a thin optically-thick model with bolometric-ish (δ³) beaming; disk colour is artistic while the physical peak temperature is shown separately in the readouts.
